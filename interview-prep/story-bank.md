## How it works

1. Every time `/career-ops oferta` generates Block F (Interview Plan), new STAR+R stories get appended here
2. Before your next interview, review this file — your stories are already organized by theme
3. The "Big Three" questions can be answered with stories from this bank:
   - "Tell me about yourself" → combine 2-3 stories into a narrative
   - "Tell me about your most impactful project" → pick your highest-impact story
   - "Tell me about a conflict you resolved" → find a story with a Reflection

### [Delivery / Change Management] MMI Contact Center Transformation — Zero Downtime Go-Live
**Source:** Report #002 — The New York Times — VP of Engineering, Wirecutter
**S (Situation):** Legacy contact center serving 100K+ consumers needed full platform replacement. The existing system was the operational core of the business — no tolerance for downtime.
**T (Task):** Lead the migration with zero service disruption on day-one go-live, while introducing AI-assisted routing, coaching/monitoring, and knowledge bots.
**A (Action):** Designed phased rollout with parallel run periods, real-time monitoring, and clear stakeholder alignment across operations and IT. Built rollback protocols before launch. Ran silent cutover during low-traffic window.
**R (Result):** Zero downtime on day-one go-live. AI-assisted routing and coaching deployed post-launch with measurable improvement in agent performance.
**Reflection:** Speed is not the enemy of quality — the discipline of the migration process made the go-live boring in the best possible way. "Boring" is what success looks like in production.
**Best for questions about:** change management, risk management, AI in production, delivery under pressure, cross-functional coordination

### [Process Intelligence / Agentic AI] AI-Assisted Contact Center — Process Optimization at Scale
**Source:** Report #003/#004 — Celonis — Field CTO
**S (Situation):** MMI's contact center handled hundreds of thousands of consumer financial counseling interactions annually. The organization evaluated AI as a means to improve throughput and agent effectiveness — but the vendor market was full of AI-washing that made genuine capability assessment difficult.
**T (Task):** Evaluate, select, and implement AI-assisted operations across the contact center — distinguishing automation from genuine AI capability, and ensuring the technology would survive production at scale.
**A (Action):** Built an evaluation framework that separated intelligent routing (automation), AI-assisted coaching and monitoring (genuine ML inference on conversation quality), and knowledge retrieval (augmented search). **Reflection:** The hardest part of an AI implementation isn't the technology — it's helping the organization understand what they actually bought. Most contact center AI deployments underperform because the buying decision conflated automation with intelligence. Naming that distinction before deployment is the difference between a successful go-live and a disappointed sponsor.
**Best for questions about:** Agentic AI, process intelligence, AI in regulated environments, C-suite advisory on AI, platform evaluation, enterprise AI adoption, Field CTO scope

### [Cloud / Compliance] Multi-Year Cloud Migration with 100% PCI-DSS Maintained
**Source:** Report #003/#004 — Celonis — Field CTO
**S (Situation):** MMI operated in a regulated financial services environment with continuous PCI-DSS certification requirements. Legacy on-premises infrastructure was reaching end-of-life, but the cost of a compliance gap was severe — regulatory, reputational, and operational.
**T (Task):** Lead a multi-year cloud-first migration without a single compliance gap or audit finding.
**A (Action):** Architected the migration in compliance-first phases. Treated PCI-DSS scope not as a project constraint but as an architectural requirement — meaning every infrastructure decision was evaluated against compliance posture from day one, not audited at the end. Selected cloud partners with native PCI-DSS controls. Built continuous audit readiness into monitoring and governance rather than treating compliance as a periodic event.
**R (Result):** 100% PCI-DSS certification maintained throughout full migration lifecycle. Zero audit findings. Complete cloud infrastructure achieved on schedule.
**Reflection:** Compliance is usually treated as a speed constraint. We treated it as a design pattern. That framing produced better architecture and faster audits. The organizations that treat compliance as an afterthought are the ones that fail audits.
**Best for questions about:** regulated industries, cloud architecture, risk management, enterprise architecture methodology, ERP-adjacent compliance, Field CTO credibility in financial services

### [AI Evaluation / Production Depth] Separating Demo Performance from Production Performance
**Source:** Report #005 — Glia — Head of AI – Product Leader
**S (Situation):** During AI architecture consulting engagements for a national CX firm, vendors whose systems performed well in demos consistently underdelivered in production — particularly in real-time voice AI scenarios with strict latency, orchestration, and quality constraints.
**T (Task):** Build an evaluation methodology that specifically tested production-level performance, not just capability demonstrations.
**A (Action):** Designed evaluation criteria around orchestration reliability, latency tradeoffs at volume, failure mode documentation, quality measurement in live environments, and agentic system behavior under edge cases. Framed the evaluation explicitly around "how does this fail?" rather than "how well does it perform?"
**R (Result):** Framework adopted as standard methodology. The core insight became the primary differentiator of the advisory practice — and the methodology was directly applicable to Glia's own emphasis on evaluation, reliability, and quality measurement in its AI product org.
**Reflection:** "The primary danger is no longer that the tool will make mistakes — it's that you'll accept them." AI product quality is not about preventing all failures — it's about building the measurement infrastructure to surface and address them continuously.
**Best for questions about:** AI evaluation rigor, production reliability, model quality, AI product operations, agentic AI deployment, contact center AI, voice AI constraints, eval frameworks

### [Build vs. Partner] AI Vendor Strategy in a Regulated Environment
**Source:** Report #005 — Glia — Head of AI – Product Leader
**S (Situation):** At MMI, multiple technology decisions involved choosing between building proprietary capabilities and partnering with external platforms — particularly for contact center AI, where the vendor landscape was changing rapidly and every decision carried compliance implications (PCI-DSS).
**T (Task):** Establish a consistent, defensible framework for build vs. partner decisions that could survive board-level scrutiny and be communicated in business terms.
**A (Action):** Built decision criteria around core competency alignment, vendor ecosystem maturity, switching cost analysis, vendor compliance posture, and total cost of ownership over a 3–5 year horizon. Applied the framework to contact center AI platform selection — ultimately choosing to partner based on vendor maturity and compliance readiness, rather than building proprietary.
**R (Result):** Consistent executive and board alignment on technology investment decisions. Avoided vendor lock-in in two major platform categories. Maintained 100% PCI-DSS certification through all transitions.
**Reflection:** The question is never "build or buy." It's "what do we need to own to remain differentiated — and what can we stop owning?" That reframe turns a cost conversation into a strategy conversation. This is exactly the frame Glia needs for its own build vs. partner decisions across its AI ecosystem.
**Best for questions about:** Build vs. buy, AI vendor strategy, platform decisions, proprietary vs. ecosystem, board-level technology communication, regulated environment strategy
